Understanding Ethical Impact Analysis 🌐

Our interactive radar chart visualizes the ripple-effects of your choices across eight ethical dimensions. Rather than labeling answers "right" or "wrong," it highlights trade-offs so you can debate, iterate, and learn.

  • 🎯 Real-Time Ethics Visualization — see scores update instantly as you make decisions
  • 📊 Multi-Dimensional Analysis — notice how boosting privacy might reduce transparency, or vice-versa
  • 🔍 Educational Discovery — there's no perfect shape; use the chart to compare ethical frameworks in context
  • ⚖️ Balanced Decision Making — understand the ethical implications of your choices before implementing them

What am I looking at? Each axis represents one ethical dimension (see below). A larger area along an axis indicates a stronger positive impact in that domain. Scenarios rarely maximize all axes; ethical reasoning is about balancing them thoughtfully.

Ethical Dimensions Overview 📚

The ethical radar chart in SimulateAI visualizes how choices impact eight interrelated ethical dimensions. Understanding these helps users interpret trade-offs and explore richer decision-making. Each dimension interacts with others, creating complex ethical landscapes that mirror real-world decision-making.

Dimension Description Key Considerations
Autonomy Respect for individuals' decision-making and control over their lives Informed consent, freedom of choice, self-determination
Fairness Promotion of justice, equity, and non-discrimination Distributional justice, procedural fairness, equal opportunity
Privacy Protection of personal boundaries and data Data collection, storage, sharing, personal control
Transparency Openness in actions, data, and reasoning Accessible information, clear communication, honest disclosure
Accountability Responsibility for decisions and consequences Clear ownership, oversight mechanisms, remedies for problems
Well-being Positive outcomes for individual and societal health Maximizing benefits, minimizing harm, stakeholder welfare
Sustainability Long-term viability and environmental stewardship Resource consumption, environmental impact, future generations
Inclusivity Recognition and integration of diverse perspectives Cultural sensitivity, accessibility, representation

📘 Real-World Example

Scenario: Implementing facial recognition in a school

  • High Accountability: Enhanced oversight and security monitoring
  • Moderate Transparency: Clear policies but complex technical implementation
  • Low Privacy: Continuous biometric surveillance of students
  • Ambiguous Inclusivity: Depends on training data bias and cultural considerations

This example shows how a single decision can have varied impacts across dimensions, requiring careful consideration of trade-offs.

Fairness

Treats all individuals and groups equitably, ensuring unbiased and just outcomes for everyone involved. Considers distributional justice, procedural fairness, and equal opportunity regardless of background or identity.

Sustainability

Supports long-term ecological and social well-being, considering environmental and societal impacts. Evaluates resource consumption, environmental footprint, and intergenerational responsibility.

Autonomy

Respects individual control and self-determination, preserving people's ability to make their own choices. Emphasizes informed consent, freedom of choice, and resistance to coercion or manipulation.

Beneficence

Promotes well-being and prevents harm, actively working to benefit individuals and communities. Focuses on maximizing positive outcomes while minimizing negative consequences for all stakeholders.

Transparency

Provides openness in decision-making processes, ensuring clarity about how and why decisions are made. Includes accessible information, clear communication, and honest disclosure of relevant factors.

Accountability

Ensures clear responsibility for decisions, with mechanisms for oversight and consequences for actions. Establishes who is responsible, how they can be held accountable, and what remedies exist for problems.

Privacy

Protects personal information and data rights, safeguarding individual confidentiality and control over personal data. Considers data collection, storage, sharing, and the right to control one's personal information.

Proportionality

Balances benefits against severity of impact, ensuring responses are appropriate to the situation's scale and context. Evaluates whether the means justify the ends and if the response matches the magnitude of the issue.

Important: These dimensions often interact or even conflict with each other. Ethical practice is the art of balancing them thoughtfully within specific contexts and constraints.

Framework Tagging & Comparative Ethics 🎯

Users can explore or remix scenarios by applying major philosophical frameworks. This layered lens allows ethical reasoning to move beyond "right/wrong" and into why and for whom different approaches matter.

🛡️ Virtue Ethics

Character-Based

Core Question: What kind of character or virtues does this decision encourage?

Focus: The moral character of the decision-maker and the virtues they embody

  • Compassion: Does this action show care for others' wellbeing?
  • Courage: Does this require moral bravery to do what's right?
  • Humility: Does this acknowledge limitations and seek wisdom?
  • Justice: Does this reflect fairness and respect for rights?
Example: A teacher choosing to report suspected abuse demonstrates courage and compassion, even if the immediate consequences are uncertain.

⚖️ Utilitarianism

Outcome-Based

Core Question: Which option leads to the greatest overall benefit or least harm for the most people?

Focus: Maximizing aggregate well-being and minimizing suffering

  • Consequences: What are the likely outcomes for all affected parties?
  • Scale: How many people are helped vs. harmed?
  • Intensity: How significant are the benefits and harms?
  • Distribution: Are benefits and burdens fairly shared?
Example: Implementing a vaccine mandate may restrict individual freedom but could prevent widespread illness and save lives in the broader population.

📋 Deontology

Duty-Based

Core Question: Are we upholding duties, rights, or rules regardless of outcomes?

Focus: Universal moral principles and categorical imperatives

  • Universalizability: Could this action become a universal law?
  • Human Dignity: Does this treat people as ends in themselves?
  • Rights: Does this respect fundamental human rights?
  • Duties: Does this fulfill moral obligations (honesty, promise-keeping)?
Example: Refusing to lie to protect someone, even if the lie would prevent harm, because honesty is a fundamental duty that should never be violated.

💝 Care Ethics

Relationship-Based

Core Question: How do we maintain and nurture relationships while responding to the needs of those who are vulnerable?

Focus: Relationships, interdependence, contextual care, and emotional responsiveness

  • Relationships: How does this decision affect bonds between people?
  • Vulnerability: Who is most at risk and needs protection or support?
  • Context: What are the specific circumstances and histories involved?
  • Emotional Intelligence: How do we respond to emotional needs with empathy?
Example: A nurse prioritizing extra time with an anxious patient over completing paperwork, recognizing that emotional care and human connection are as important as administrative efficiency.

🔄 Framework Integration in Practice

Rather than choosing just one framework, SimulateAI encourages users to apply all four lenses to the same scenario. This reveals different ethical dimensions and helps develop more nuanced moral reasoning. Often, frameworks may conflict—and that's where the real learning happens.

Try this: Pick any scenario and ask yourself all four questions. Notice how your reasoning changes and which aspects each framework highlights or overlooks.

  • Respectful Tone: Use language that invites exploration rather than judgment
